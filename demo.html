Troponin Kinetics
Troponin levels typically rise 3-4 hours after myocardial injury, peak at 24-48 hours, and remain elevated for 7-14 days. This makes it highly sensitive and specific for cardiac injury.

NSTEMI vs STEMI
NSTEMI represents partial coronary occlusion with subendocardial ischemia, while STEMI indicates complete occlusion with transmural infarction. Treatment urgency differs - STEMI requires immediate cath, NSTEMI within 24-48 hours if high risk.

üíé
MONA Protocol
Remember MONA for initial ACS management: Morphine (for pain), Oxygen (if hypoxic), Nitroglycerin (for chest pain/BP control), Aspirin (antiplatelet). Note: Routine O2 in normoxic patients may be harmful.
basic management
1. What is the significance of elevated troponin in this patient?
Answer: Elevated troponin indicates myocardial injury. In the context of chest pain, a level of 0.8 ng/mL (20x the upper limit of normal) strongly suggests acute myocardial infarction. The degree of elevation correlates with infarct size and prognosis.

Teaching Point: Troponin can be elevated in non-ACS conditions like PE, myocarditis, sepsis, or renal failure. Always interpret in clinical context.
2. When should this patient go for cardiac catheterization?
Answer: Immediate catheterization is not required unless STEMI or hemodynamic instability. For NSTEMI, timing depends on risk stratification. High-risk features (recurrent ischemia, hemodynamic instability, arrhythmias) warrant early invasive strategy within 24 hours.

Teaching Point: Use GRACE or TIMI scores to risk-stratify NSTEMI patients and guide timing of intervention.
3. What antiplatelet regimen would you start?
Answer: Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) with aspirin 325mg loading dose + P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel 600mg, ticagrelor 180mg, or prasugrel 60mg loading dose). Choice depends on bleeding risk, planned intervention timing, and patient factors.

Teaching Point: Ticagrelor and prasugrel are more potent than clopidogrel but carry higher bleeding risk. Avoid prasugrel if history of stroke/TIA.
